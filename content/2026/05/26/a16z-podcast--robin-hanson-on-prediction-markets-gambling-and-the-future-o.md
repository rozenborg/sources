Robin Hanson argues that prediction markets represent humanity's most powerful information aggregation tool, yet face irrational political backlash exemplified by Minnesota's recent criminalization law. He envisions expanding beyond simple forecasting to "decision markets" that could guide major choices for corporations, governments, and individuals by revealing the likely consequences of different policy options.

---

- **Minnesota criminalization** Recent state law banning prediction markets reflects broader political hostility despite their superior forecasting accuracy compared to polls and expert opinions

- **Kalshi and Polymarket resistance** Regulatory pushback against prediction market platforms stems from discomfort with gambling-adjacent activities rather than legitimate consumer protection concerns

- **Decision markets evolution** Hanson's long-term vision involves markets that don't just predict outcomes but help entities choose between alternatives by pricing the consequences of different decisions

- **Information aggregation superiority** Prediction markets consistently outperform traditional forecasting methods because they harness financial incentives to reward accurate information and punish bias

- **Institutional adoption potential** Companies and governments could use decision markets to improve strategic planning by getting market-based assessments of policy impacts before implementation

- **Psychological barriers** Public resistance to prediction markets often reflects deeper discomfort with treating serious topics as "gameable" rather than evidence-based policy concerns

- **AI integration opportunities** Future prediction markets could incorporate artificial intelligence to enhance information processing while maintaining human judgment and local knowledge
