Marc Rowan argues that private markets are fundamentally displacing traditional banking and public markets as the primary source of capital for the real economy, driven by regulatory constraints on banks and the growing complexity of modern businesses that require more sophisticated, flexible financing solutions.

---

- **Private credit revolution** Traditional banks are retreating from lending due to regulatory capital requirements, creating a massive opportunity for private credit funds to fill the $4+ trillion financing gap in the middle market

- **Public market dysfunction** Companies are staying private longer because public markets have become too focused on quarterly earnings and activist investors, while private markets offer patient capital and operational expertise

- **AI infrastructure thesis** Apollo is heavily investing in data centers and AI infrastructure, viewing the current moment as comparable to the railroad boom of the 1800s in terms of transformative capital deployment opportunities

- **Software repricing cycle** Enterprise software valuations are undergoing a major correction as companies scrutinize SaaS spending and demand better unit economics, creating distressed investment opportunities

- **Institutional adaptation imperative** Rowan emphasizes that successful organizations must continuously evolve their business models rather than defend legacy approaches, citing Apollo's evolution from buyouts to credit to retirement services

- **Scale advantages in private markets** The increasing complexity of deals and regulatory requirements is creating winner-take-all dynamics that favor large, established alternative asset managers with global reach and operational capabilities

- **Retirement crisis opportunity** Apollo is positioning itself as a solution to the $15 trillion retirement savings shortfall by bringing private market returns to individual investors through insurance partnerships and annuity products
