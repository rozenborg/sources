Modern AI development has fractured into distinct paradigms that are often conflated but serve fundamentally different purposes: philosophical workflows for institutional memory, role-based skill packs for rapid execution, autonomous research harnesses for optimization loops, process frameworks for disciplined development, and theoretical recursive improvement capabilities. Understanding these layers prevents tool misalignment and clarifies when each approach delivers maximum leverage.

---

- **Compound Engineering** is human-agent institutional memory, not automation — 80% planning/review creates durable knowledge files that compound across projects, ideal for teams shipping real products where lessons must persist beyond individual features

- **gstack** provides cold-start velocity through role delegation — inherit Garry Tan's shipping discipline via 23 specialist personas (CEO, QA, Security) that turn one human into a virtual organization producing 10-20k LOC/day

- **Autoresearch** solves the autonomous optimization problem — ~12 experiments per hour against single scalar metrics through git-based memory, proven with 700 experiments yielding 11% speedups and smaller models beating larger ones

- **Superpowers** enforces process discipline over personas — test-driven development with fresh context windows per sub-agent prevents drift when guardrails matter more than speed

- **Recursive Self-Improvement** is the theoretical umbrella — systems that improve their own improvement mechanisms, with Compound Engineering as human-in-the-loop RSI and Autoresearch as bounded model-level RSI

- **Tool selection framework** depends on your bottleneck — use gstack for fast execution, Compound Engineering for knowledge retention, Autoresearch for measurable optimization, Superpowers for process compliance

- **RSI design lens** clarifies system architecture — ask what is the loop, what gets codified, and who closes it to distinguish true self-improvement from static scaffolding

- **Strategic implication** is paradigm clarity — these aren't competing tools but different stack layers, preventing misaligned expectations when rapid prototyping workflows meet production knowledge management needs
