Microsoft's global sales organization struggled to sustain AI adoption despite having sophisticated technology and users, with daily usage plummeting from 22% to 5% within a month of Copilot's 2024 launch. The company ultimately achieved over 60% daily active usage by abandoning traditional deployment playbooks and creating experimental safe spaces, but now faces deeper questions about autonomous sales agents that can act independently rather than just assist.

---

- **Standard deployment failed** Traditional IT rollout approaches collapsed because general-purpose AI requires experimentation and discovery, unlike single-function software with clear use cases and workflows.

- **Productivity J-curve reality** Users initially lose productivity when adopting AI tools, requiring management support to protect quota-carrying salespeople during the learning dip before benefits materialize.

- **Experimentation bottleneck** High-performing veterans often resist adoption due to competency traps, while tech-savvy experimenters lack organizational credibility to drive broader adoption across teams.

- **Social proof trumps training** Pairing respected anti-tech veterans with young experimenters proved more effective than formal training programs, as peer adoption signals carry more weight than management mandates.

- **Role transformation opportunity** AI automation of administrative tasks enables salespeople to evolve from transactional processors to strategic consultants, fundamentally upgrading their value proposition to customers.

- **Autonomous agent dilemma** Next-generation AI that acts independently rather than assists creates new governance challenges around customer relationships, brand risk, and the boundaries of human judgment in sales.

- **Scaling governance questions** Organizations must determine whether autonomous agents should be centrally managed or distributed to individual salespeople who understand their specific customer relationships and preferences.
