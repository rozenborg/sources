America's defense industrial base faces a critical mismatch between Cold War-era production methods and the scale demands of modern great power competition, requiring fundamental shifts toward software-first platforms, autonomous manufacturing, and commercial market integration to achieve the speed and volume necessary for deterrence.

---

- **Production capacity crisis** - Current defense manufacturing operates at artisanal scale when potential conflicts demand industrial-level production volumes, creating a strategic vulnerability that existing shipyards and factories cannot address

- **Software-first platform design** - Building military systems around software and autonomy from the ground up enables cheaper, more adaptable platforms while reducing the complexity and cost of traditional heavily-manned systems

- **Autonomous manufacturing imperative** - Modern production must shift toward automated processes and digital manufacturing to achieve the speed and scale required, moving away from labor-intensive traditional methods

- **Commercial market leverage** - Defense capabilities increasingly depend on commercial technology and supply chains, making it essential to align private sector incentives with national security priorities rather than relying solely on defense contractors

- **DoD procurement reform needed** - The Department of Defense must restructurally change how it works with industry, moving beyond traditional contracting approaches to enable rapid scaling and innovation

- **Supply chain fragility exposure** - Existing defense supply chains are brittle and concentrated, requiring diversification and resilience planning to withstand disruption during extended competition or conflict

- **Capital alignment challenge** - Long-term national security depends on channeling private investment toward defense-relevant capabilities, requiring new mechanisms to make such investments attractive to commercial capital
