Major tech companies are racing to develop AI-native devices that could potentially dethrone the smartphone, with OpenAI partnering with former Apple designer Jony Ive, Meta betting on AR glasses, and Amazon exploring wearable AI assistants. However, despite billions in investment and ambitious prototypes, current attempts like Humane's AI Pin and Rabbit's R1 have failed spectacularly due to battery limitations, connectivity issues, and inability to match the smartphone's integrated ecosystem of apps and services.

---

- **Market plateau signals opportunity** - Smartphone sales dropped 19% between 2017-2024, with users holding devices longer and buying refurbished alternatives, creating an opening for disruptive technology that can offer compelling advantages over incremental upgrades

- **The "AI body problem" drives innovation** - Traditional touchscreen interfaces limit AI's potential for natural voice commands, gestures, and ambient computing, forcing probabilistic AI systems into rigid app-based channels not designed for adaptive intelligence

- **High-profile partnerships emerging** - OpenAI's $6.5 billion collaboration with Jony Ive's LoveForm studio aims to create screenless, context-aware devices by 2026, while rumors suggest an "AI agent phone" could enter mass production by 2027 with projected 30 million unit shipments

- **Early attempts reveal major technical barriers** - Humane's $699 AI Pin and Rabbit's $199 R1 both flopped due to overheating, poor battery life, lack of app integration, and unclear value propositions, highlighting the gap between prototype concepts and daily-use reliability

- **Form factor experiments span wearables to implants** - Companies are exploring smart glasses with EMG wristbands (Meta Orion), conversation-transcribing pendants (Amazon Bee), and even brain-computer interfaces (Neuralink), but none yet solve the fundamental power, connectivity, and ecosystem challenges

- **Privacy concerns may limit adoption** - Always-listening devices with cameras and biometric sensors raise significant legal and psychological barriers, particularly for ambient computing that continuously processes environmental data and personal interactions

- **Ecosystem lock-in remains strongest defense** - Two decades of iOS/Android app development, user habits, and integrated services create massive switching costs that any replacement device must overcome while offering compelling unique capabilities smartphones cannot provide
