David Reich's new research demolishes the scientific consensus that natural selection has been largely dormant in humans since agriculture began, revealing instead that genetic evolution accelerated dramatically during the Bronze Age 3,000 years ago. His data shows cognitive ability genes experienced their strongest selection pressure between 4,000-2,000 years ago, increasing by roughly one standard deviation, while he also proposes a heretical new model suggesting Neanderthals were genetically-swamped modern humans rather than a separate archaic lineage.

---

- **Selection acceleration discovery** - Ancient DNA analysis reveals natural selection sped up rather than slowed down over the last 10,000 years, contradicting long-held scientific consensus about post-agricultural human evolution

- **Bronze Age genetic upheaval** - Around 3,000 years ago, gene frequencies for immune function, body fat, and intelligence were in maximum flux, marking this period as an evolutionary inflection point

- **Cognitive enhancement timeline** - Genetic predictors of cognitive performance increased by approximately one full standard deviation over 10,000 years, with most gains concentrated between 4,000-2,000 years ago

- **Neanderthal revision theory** - Reich proposes Neanderthals weren't a separate archaic lineage but rather modern humans who expanded from the Caucasus 300,000 years ago and became genetically swamped by European archaic populations

- **Shared cultural ancestry** - The new model suggests Neanderthals and modern humans share the same Middle Stone Age technological and cultural origins, differing only in which archaic populations they subsequently mixed with

- **Methodological breakthrough** - The findings required scaling ancient DNA sequencing and developing new statistical methods to detect selection patterns previously invisible to researchers

- **Time versus population constraints** - Research suggests evolutionary limitations stem from time constraints rather than population size, challenging assumptions about what factors limit human genetic change
