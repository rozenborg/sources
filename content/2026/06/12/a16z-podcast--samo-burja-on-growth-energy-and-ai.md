AI development is creating a massive demand shock that will drive industrial expansion across energy, manufacturing, and construction sectors, potentially triggering the first major wave of physical infrastructure growth in decades. Burja argues this represents a fundamental shift from software-focused tech growth to hardware-intensive industrial capacity building that will reshape global supply chains and economic power dynamics.

---

- **Infrastructure demand shock** AI's computational requirements are driving unprecedented demand for data centers, energy generation, and manufacturing capacity, creating opportunities beyond traditional tech sectors

- **Energy bottleneck reality** The power requirements for AI training and deployment are forcing serious conversations about nuclear energy, grid modernization, and industrial-scale electricity generation that governments can no longer defer

- **Manufacturing renaissance potential** AI demand could reverse decades of manufacturing decline in developed nations as proximity to compute infrastructure and reliable energy becomes strategically critical

- **Institutional capacity advantage** Countries and organizations with strong state capacity to coordinate large-scale infrastructure projects will have decisive advantages over those relying purely on market mechanisms

- **China-US competition dynamics** The AI race is fundamentally about industrial capacity and resource mobilization rather than just algorithmic breakthroughs, favoring nations that can effectively organize physical resources

- **Demographic constraints binding** Declining birth rates in developed nations create structural limitations on growth and institutional maintenance that automation may not fully offset

- **Welfare state sustainability crisis** Current social safety nets were designed for different demographic and economic conditions and face fundamental restructuring as automation reshapes labor markets
