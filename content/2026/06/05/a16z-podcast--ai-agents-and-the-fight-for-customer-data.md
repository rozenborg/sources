Fivetran's CEO George Fraser argues that while AI agents will reshape how users interact with enterprise software, they won't eliminate the need for centralized data infrastructure—instead creating new demand for unified data platforms as companies resist giving agents direct access to critical systems of record.

---

- **Data centralization remains critical** - Despite AI agents promising direct system access, enterprises will likely funnel agent queries through centralized data warehouses rather than risk exposing production systems to potential AI errors or security breaches

- **Fivetran-dbt merger strategy** - The combination creates an end-to-end data pipeline from extraction to transformation, positioning the merged company to capture more value as data becomes increasingly central to AI workflows

- **Enterprise AI adoption lag** - Fraser believes most predictions about AI disrupting enterprise software are overly aggressive, with large organizations moving much slower than consumer markets due to risk tolerance and operational complexity

- **Agent access backlash anticipated** - Companies will push back against AI agents directly querying systems of record, creating demand for "agent-safe" data environments that provide necessary information without production system exposure

- **Data gravity intensifies** - As AI applications proliferate, the pull toward centralized data platforms strengthens because agents need comprehensive, clean datasets rather than fragmented point solutions

- **Coding agents show early promise** - Fraser sees software development as one of the first enterprise use cases where AI agents demonstrate clear productivity gains, potentially accelerating data infrastructure development cycles

- **Open data architecture wins** - Companies that maintain flexible, accessible data foundations will better capitalize on AI opportunities compared to those locked into proprietary or siloed systems
