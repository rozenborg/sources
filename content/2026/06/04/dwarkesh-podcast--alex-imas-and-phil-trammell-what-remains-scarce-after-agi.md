Two prominent economists argue that contrary to popular assumptions, AGI may not lead to economic collapse or mass unemployment, but instead could create a world where human labor becomes economically irrelevant while capital owners capture unprecedented wealth shares. The key insight: even if AI makes everything cheaper, fundamental scarcities like land, natural resources, and positional goods will ensure markets continue functioning, though with radically different wealth distribution patterns than today.

---

- **Capital share explosion** AGI will likely increase capital's share of economic output far beyond today's ~30%, potentially reaching 70-90%, as AI-powered production becomes vastly more efficient than human labor across most economic sectors

- **Demand won't collapse** Despite AI replacing workers, aggregate demand will persist because capital owners will spend their massive wealth gains on scarce goods like prime real estate, natural resources, and luxury positional goods that remain inherently limited

- **Redistribution complexity** Taxing AI wealth will face novel challenges - traditional labor income taxes become irrelevant, requiring new frameworks focused on capital gains, wealth taxes, or universal basic income funded by AI productivity gains

- **Geographic inequality** Countries outside the AI supply chain face potential economic marginalization unless they develop strategies to capture AI gains through sovereign wealth funds, international taxation agreements, or leveraging remaining comparative advantages

- **Human labor obsolescence** Even in optimistic scenarios, integrating human workers into an AI-dominated economy becomes increasingly difficult as the productivity gap widens, making humans economically uncompetitive rather than complementary

- **Intrinsic wealth accumulation** If humans or AIs develop preferences for wealth accumulation as an end in itself (not just consumption), this could drive continued economic growth and inequality even in post-scarcity scenarios

- **Policy timing critical** The "messy middle" period during AI deployment may offer the best window for implementing redistribution mechanisms before AI capabilities become so advanced that capital owners have overwhelming political and economic leverage
