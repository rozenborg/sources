Axiom Math's CEO Carina Hong argues that formal verification—not just coding ability—represents the critical bottleneck preventing AI systems from achieving AGI, as evidenced by her startup's dominance on math benchmarks (12/12 Putnam problems vs OpenAI o3's 4.9% on ProofGen) through verified generation techniques. While Anthropic's Claude Code gains market momentum through informal coding prowess, Hong contends that verified AI systems using languages like Lean create exponentially stronger training signals and compounding knowledge bases that informal statistical methods cannot match.

---

- **Putnam dominance signals verification advantage** Axiom solved all 12 problems on the prestigious undergraduate math exam and achieved 99% (187/189) on the ProofGen benchmark, vastly outperforming OpenAI o3's 4.9% success rate

- **Verification creates superior training loops** Using Lean proof verification provides deterministic reward signals during reinforcement learning, replacing statistical guesses with mathematical certainty and enabling higher sample efficiency

- **Compounding knowledge thesis** Formally verified proofs create trusted training data that accumulates over time, allowing future models to build reliably on previous work rather than starting from scratch with informal reasoning

- **Frontier labs still using informal methods** Despite verification's apparent advantages, major AI companies continue training on informal proofs rather than generating Lean code directly, potentially leaving performance gains untapped

- **Specification remains the bottleneck** While Lean proofs are expensive to generate but cheap to verify, the core challenge shifts to properly specifying what problems we want solved in the first place

- **Cross-domain verification trend emerging** From hardware control systems to theoretical physics, the need for verifiable AI outputs is becoming critical as model capabilities exceed human ability to manually check results

- **Hardware verification as killer application** Formal verification of complex systems like flight controls and nuclear plants represents a high-value commercial opportunity as software complexity increases
