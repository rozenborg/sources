AI-enabled cyberattacks are rapidly evolving beyond traditional detection methods, with 67% of malicious actors now using AI for malware development and threat sophistication increasing 1.7-fold over a 12-month period. Current cybersecurity frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK are failing to capture the most dangerous new attack patterns, particularly autonomous AI agents that can orchestrate multi-stage attacks with minimal human oversight.

---

- **Malware automation dominance** 560 of 832 banned accounts (67.3%) used AI primarily for writing malware, while only 6.5% leveraged AI for advanced lateral movement within compromised networks

- **Threat escalation acceleration** Medium-to-high risk attackers increased from 33% to 56% between the first and second six-month periods, representing a 1.7-fold jump in sophistication

- **Attack lifecycle evolution** AI usage shifted from initial access techniques toward post-compromise activities, with account discovery rising 8.9% while AI-assisted phishing fell 8.6%

- **Traditional risk indicators obsolete** Low-skilled actors now employ an average of 16 distinct techniques compared to 20 for high-skilled actors, making technique count unreliable for threat assessment

- **Autonomous orchestration gap** The most dangerous attacks involve AI agents making real-time tactical decisions with minimal human input, but these behaviors lack corresponding MITRE ATT&CK classifications

- **Framework inadequacy demonstrated** A state-sponsored operation using autonomous Claude Code agents scored maximum risk (100) despite employing only 30 techniques across 13 tactics—numbers typical of medium-risk actors

- **Defensive response deployment** Anthropic has implemented new cyber safeguards on their models to detect malware development and mass data exfiltration, while expanding Project Glasswing to 150 organizations across 15+ countries
