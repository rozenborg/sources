Organizations are bleeding competitive advantage as employees hoard personally-developed AI expertise, with workers who've mastered effective prompting techniques and workflows choosing to keep their knowledge private despite colleagues struggling with the same tools.

---

- **Knowledge hoarding epidemic** A physician developed a highly effective prompting template for his organization's approved AI tool but hasn't shared it, even knowing colleagues are struggling with poor results from the same platform

- **Private experimentation advantage** Employees are conducting personal AI experiments outside formal channels, developing valuable workflows and techniques that become individual intellectual property rather than organizational assets

- **Governance gap misdiagnosis** The transparency problem isn't primarily driven by weak AI governance policies but by employee choice and incentive structures that reward individual expertise over knowledge sharing

- **Competitive disadvantage multiplication** Organizations lose twice—first by not capturing innovations their own employees develop, then by having inconsistent AI performance across teams using identical tools

- **Skills inequality within teams** Even when organizations provide approved AI tools, performance varies dramatically based on individual employees' private learning and template development

- **Institutional knowledge risk** Valuable AI methodologies remain locked in individual employees' personal systems, creating succession planning vulnerabilities and limiting organizational AI maturity

- **Intervention opportunity** Companies could implement knowledge-sharing incentives and structured AI learning programs to capture and distribute employee-developed AI expertise across the organization
