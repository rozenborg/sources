## Summary

- METR's "exponential time horizon" chart became widely misinterpreted on social media, generating millions of impressions without proper context about nuances, disclaimers, and error bars

- The Long Horizons evaluation framework has known biases that authors disclosed but are underappreciated in public discourse about AI capabilities

- Time horizon evaluations measure how long AI systems can work autonomously on complex tasks before requiring human intervention

- Task selection for evaluations introduces inherent biases - researchers must balance between tasks that are too easy (providing no signal) and too hard (also providing no signal)

- Current productivity studies show mixed results - most participants see minimal gains from AI assistance, with rare outliers achieving significant productivity improvements

- Compute scaling may be hitting diminishing returns, but algorithmic improvements still require substantial computational resources to implement effectively

- Industry spending on AI training runs continues to increase dramatically, with major clusters taking 12-18 months from planning to deployment

- Prediction markets for AI model capabilities could provide valuable forecasting mechanisms, though current platforms have limitations in market depth and participation

- Beyond traditional benchmarks, METR is developing "threat evaluation" frameworks to assess potential risks from advanced AI systems

- Developer productivity measurements reveal that AI coding assistants benefit only a small fraction of users significantly, challenging assumptions about universal productivity gains

- Strategic implication: The gap between AI hype and measured reality remains substantial, requiring more sophisticated evaluation frameworks

- Actionable insight: Organizations should focus on identifying the specific use cases and user profiles where AI tools provide genuine productivity improvements rather than assuming universal benefits
