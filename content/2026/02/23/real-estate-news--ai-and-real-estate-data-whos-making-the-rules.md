## Summary

## Key Facts

- California passed legislation requiring disclosure of AI-altered listing photos with side-by-side comparisons
- MLSs are adopting "license, not lawsuit" strategies to update data governance before litigation emerges
- Current MLS policies (IDX/VOW frameworks) were written before AI integration and need modernization
- Real estate industry already contains sensitive data: buyer details, showing schedules, financial information
- Some brokerages (e.g., eXp) have updated listing agreements with AI disclosure language and agent training

## Strategic Implications

- AI scales existing risks rather than creating new ones - manipulation becomes faster, cheaper, easier
- State real estate commissions may emerge as primary regulatory authority over national trade groups
- MLSs are evolving from listing databases to secure infrastructure providers (similar to AWS model)
- Data privacy will likely become the next major litigation flashpoint
- Industry seeks to establish rules proactively rather than reactively through court decisions

## Actionable Insights

- **For MLSs**: Update data agreements to focus on prohibited uses rather than approved applications list
- **For Brokerages**: Implement AI disclosure protocols and agent training programs immediately
- **For Agents**: Avoid entering sensitive client documents (contracts, inspection reports, underwriting) into commercial AI tools
- **For Industry Leaders**: Engage with state real estate commissions on regulatory frameworks before federal intervention
- **For Legal Teams**: Establish clear liability frameworks for AI-generated errors before widespread adoption
- **For Technology Vendors**: Build compliance features into AI tools rather than relying on post-deployment governance
