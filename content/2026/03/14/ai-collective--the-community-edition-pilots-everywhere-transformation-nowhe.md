Harvard Business School's Frontier Firm Initiative found that enterprise AI transformation is stalling on seven specific organizational frictions rather than technical limitations, with companies running hundreds of pilots that never scale to standard operations. Meanwhile, the widely-cited ATM automation story that ends with stable bank teller employment actually continues through 2010 when mobile banking eliminated 168,000 jobs by making branches irrelevant — suggesting AI's real impact may come from enabling entirely new organizational forms rather than task automation.

---

- **Scaling Crisis** Investment banks are running 250+ LLM applications with zero scaled to standard operating procedures, while payment networks see double-digit productivity gains that never appear on balance sheets due to organizational absorption

- **Governance Breakdown** Human-in-the-loop controls designed for sequential workflows collapse under multi-agent architectures, with global banks planning deployments from 100+ agents to tens of thousands without adequate oversight frameworks

- **Process Debt Exposure** AI surfaces decades of undocumented tribal knowledge and broken workflows before it can fix them, creating resistance from knowledge holders whose expertise forms their professional identity

- **Platform Complexity Reset** Teams restart implementation cycles every time new models drop, preventing institutional learning and creating perpetual pilot mode across enterprise deployments

- **Automation vs Displacement** The ATM story's real lesson isn't task substitution (which held teller jobs steady through 2010) but platform displacement — the iPhone eliminated 168,000 banking jobs by making branches optional, not more efficient

- **Browser-Native Agent Infrastructure** Google, MCP-B, and HumanX are building inference capabilities and protocols that treat browsers as first-class agent deployment surfaces rather than just UI layers, enabling new organizational architectures

- **Practitioner-Led Implementation** Successful AI deployment requires scoping specific problems before automation, as demonstrated by Bogotá's 50-person brewery session focusing on qualified opportunity generation rather than volume creation in sales workflows
