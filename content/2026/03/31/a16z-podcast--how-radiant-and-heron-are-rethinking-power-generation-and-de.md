Radiant is building portable nuclear reactors while Heron focuses on power delivery infrastructure, with both companies targeting the core constraint that power delivery—not generation—has become the primary bottleneck in America's energy system. Their modular, manufactured approaches could unlock distributed energy deployment at scale, particularly as AI data centers create massive new power demands that traditional grid infrastructure cannot efficiently serve.

---

- **Nuclear portability breakthrough** Radiant's micro reactors can be transported by truck and deployed in remote locations, using helium cooling and particle fuel to eliminate traditional nuclear infrastructure requirements

- **Power delivery bottleneck** Grid interconnection queues now stretch 4-5 years, making power delivery the limiting factor rather than generation capacity—a fundamental shift in energy economics  

- **Manufacturing over construction** Both companies emphasize factory-built, modular systems that can scale through manufacturing learning curves rather than bespoke construction projects

- **Data center grid impact** Hyperscale data centers can actually stabilize grids by providing flexible, interruptible loads that help balance renewable intermittency, contrary to common assumptions about their grid burden

- **Solid state power revolution** Heron's silicon carbide-based power electronics enable more efficient power conversion and grid integration, particularly crucial for renewable energy sources

- **Regulatory pathway clarity** Nuclear regulations are becoming more accommodating to innovative reactor designs, while power delivery regulations remain the more complex challenge

- **Distributed energy future** The combination of portable generation and advanced power electronics enables energy infrastructure that can be deployed closer to demand sources, reducing transmission losses and grid stress
