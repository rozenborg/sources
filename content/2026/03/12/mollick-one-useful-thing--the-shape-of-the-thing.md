AI has crossed into a new era where agents can complete hours of human work in minutes, creating "rolling disruption" as exponential capability improvements unlock radical organizational changes like software factories that code without human involvement. This shift from co-intelligence to managing AI represents a fundamental transformation in work itself, accelerated by recursive self-improvement where AI systems increasingly build better versions of themselves.

---

- **Capability acceleration** AI performance has improved exponentially across all major benchmarks since 2022, with systems now matching or exceeding human experts on complex tasks 82% of the time and scoring 94% on graduate-level knowledge tests

- **Agent revolution** New AI agents can autonomously complete multi-hour tasks in minutes, shifting human roles from collaborative prompting to delegating and managing AI work output

- **Organizational experimentation** Companies like StrongDM are implementing radical "software factories" where AI agents write, test, and ship production code without human review, spending $1,000+ per engineer daily on AI compute

- **Market volatility preview** A single February 2025 week demonstrated the new reality: fictional AI scenarios triggered major stock movements, companies announced AI-driven layoffs, and government-AI conflicts emerged over control and governance

- **Recursive self-improvement reality** All major AI labs are actively working to close the loop where AI systems build better versions of themselves, potentially steepening exponential improvement curves with uncertain endpoints

- **Window for influence** Organizations experimenting with AI integration now are setting precedents for everyone else, creating a brief but critical opportunity to shape how this technology transforms work and society

- **Predictable unpredictability** The combination of threshold-crossing capabilities, organizational experimentation, and policy conflicts will create ongoing waves of sudden, disruptive changes across markets and institutions
