Scientific discovery often survives decades of making worse predictions than existing theories through poorly understood judgment and heuristics that resist codification into AI systems. Terence Tao argues that while AI accelerates certain aspects of mathematical research by making papers "richer and broader," it fails to achieve the deeper conceptual breakthroughs that define genuine scientific progress.

---

- **Kepler's discovery paradox** - Planetary motion laws emerged through a messy, high-temperature process where the correct theory initially made worse predictions than existing models, surviving only through intuitive judgment
- **Verification loop limitations** - Unlike AI's tight feedback loops, major scientific breakthroughs often require decades or millennia of validation, challenging assumptions about AI's discovery advantages
- **AI research quality trade-offs** - Current AI tools make mathematical papers broader and more comprehensive but fail to generate the deeper insights that drive fundamental progress
- **Deductive overhang problem** - There may be vast amounts of discoverable knowledge using existing methods before we need genuinely new conceptual frameworks
- **Human-AI hybrid dominance** - Mathematical research will likely remain dominated by human-AI collaboration rather than pure AI systems for an extended period
- **Semi-formal language gap** - Scientific progress requires developing better formal languages that capture how scientists actually communicate, bridging informal intuition and rigid formalization
- **Selection bias in AI discoveries** - Reported AI breakthroughs may reflect cherry-picked successes rather than systematic capabilities, masking true limitations in scientific reasoning
